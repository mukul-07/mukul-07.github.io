A Note on Building Things

The one you'd recommend
to your mother.

In a single, unrehearsed answer to a journalist, Steve Jobs gave the simplest definition of "product" anyone has ever offered. Two decades later, it's still the only one that matters.

By Mukul Dharpure Originally pub. Aug 16, 2023 Edited & illustrated

Most of us are quietly curious about how the people running companies actually think — what keeps them up at night, what they argue about in rooms we're not in, and how they decide whether the thing they've built is good enough to put in front of a stranger. After two years of working closely with a CEO, the lesson I keep returning to is unglamorous and a little inconvenient: defining a product is genuinely hard.

Look around your desk right now. The objects you reach for without thinking. The apps you open without complaint. Some of them feel inevitable — as though they couldn't have been any other way. Others feel like compromises someone shipped because the quarter was ending. We all know the difference. We just rarely articulate it.

And almost all of us, somewhere on that desk, have something made by Apple.

✦ ✦ ✦

A question, and an answer.

Steve Jobs is, by most reasonable accounts, the most consequential product builder of the last half-century. His definition of what a product is wasn't written down in a manifesto. It came out, almost casually, in an interview where a journalist tried to push him into a corner.

Interviewer

"There has been a suggestion that because of pricing and design, Apple tends to appeal to a smaller elite rather than a mass customer base — so, is it your goal to overtake the PC in market share?"

Steve Jobs

"I can tell you what our goal is. Our goal is to make the best personal computers in the world, and to make products we are proud to sell and would recommend to our family and friends. And we want to do that at the lowest price we can."

"But I have to tell you — there's some stuff in our industry that we wouldn't be proud to ship, that we wouldn't be proud to recommend to our family and friends, and we can't do it. We just can't ship junk. So there are thresholds we can't cross because of who we are."

Notice what he didn't say. He didn't say "biggest." He didn't say "most profitable." He didn't say "fastest growing." He named three things, in this order: best, recommendable, cheapest possible. And then he drew a line nobody asked him to draw — the line where pride ends and shame begins.

The Anatomy of His Answer

Three pillars hold up one definition.

Strip Jobs' reply of its rhetoric and three load-bearing commitments remain. Remove any one and what stands above collapses.

The Family & Friends Test.

The middle pillar is the one most people miss, because it sounds like sentimentality. It isn't. It's the most ruthless quality bar in the building.

Customers are abstract. Markets are abstract. "Users" are abstract. But your mother is not abstract. Your best friend is not abstract. The moment you imagine handing them the box, the question stops being "will this sell?" and becomes "will I still respect myself on Sunday when they call me with a problem?"

That is a different question. It is harder to answer. And it is impossible to answer dishonestly for very long.

Fig. 02 — The Threshold

A Line You Can't Cross

The shape of "who we are."

Most companies have a quality floor — the lowest thing they'll allow out the door. Jobs flipped it: a pride ceiling, below which you simply do not exist. Everything below the line is, in his word, junk.

Why this is strategy, not sentiment.

Read the answer one more time and notice the order. Best comes first. Pride comes second. Price comes last — and even then, qualified: "the lowest price we can." Not the lowest price. The lowest price that doesn't compromise the first two.

Most companies build in the opposite order. They start with a price point — what the market will bear, what the spreadsheet demands — and then work backwards into whatever quality survives the budget. Jobs is describing a product strategy that starts at the top and asks the spreadsheet to keep up.

Fig. 03 — In Practice

The Test, Applied

One question, asked before the launch.

A practical decision flow distilled from the Jobs definition. The whole apparatus pivots on a single, uncomfortable question — and most projects fail it on the first ask.

None of this is a slogan. It's an operating instruction. The pillars tell you what you're optimizing for. The threshold tells you what you'll never trade away. The question tells you how to decide, today, before the launch slides go up. Most product debates resolve themselves the moment someone in the room asks it honestly.

The reason Jobs' definition has aged so well is that it's portable. You can use it on a phone, on a feature, on a sentence in an onboarding email. Held against the test, most things shrink. The few that survive tend to be the ones worth your name.

A product, in the end, is the one you'd recommend to your family and friends.
Everything else is just inventory.
